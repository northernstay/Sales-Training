📋 Independent Contractor — What It Means & Your Tax Obligations

Working with Northern Stay means you are an independent contractor, not an employee. This is an important distinction that affects how you get paid, how you handle taxes, and what you're responsible for. Read this carefully.

⚖️ Employee vs. Independent Contractor — The Key Differences

💸 Taxes — What You Need to Know
No tax is withheld from your commissions. You receive your full 3.8% — but you owe income tax on it. Set money aside from every payment.
Rule of thumb: Set aside 25–30% of every commission payment for taxes. This covers federal/provincial income tax and your CPP contributions.
T4A slip: At year end, Northern Stay will issue you a T4A showing your total earnings. You'll use this to file your return.
Tax installments: If you owe more than $3,000 in taxes in a year, the CRA may require you to pay quarterly installments the following year rather than a lump sum at filing time.
🧾 Business Expenses You Can Deduct
As a self-employed contractor, you can deduct eligible expenses against your income — reducing your taxable amount. Common deductions include:
→ Portion of your phone/internet bill used for work
→ Home office expenses (if you work from home)
→ Office supplies, headset, computer equipment
→ Professional development costs
→ Bank fees on a business account
→ Accounting or tax preparation fees
Keep receipts for everything. The CRA can audit up to 6 years back.
📝 How to File Your Taxes as a Self-Employed Contractor in Canada
1
Track your income all year. Keep a record of every commission payment received. Your T4A from Northern Stay will confirm your total — but don't rely on it as your only record.
2
Keep all your business receipts. Phone bills, internet, equipment, home office — anything work-related. Physical or digital, organized by month.
3
Check if you need to register for GST/HST. If your self-employment income (from all sources) exceeds $30,000 in a calendar year, you're required to register for a GST/HST number and collect it on your services. Under $30,000 you're a "small supplier" and exempt — but you can register voluntarily to claim input tax credits.
4
File a T1 General return with a T2125. Self-employment income is reported on Form T2125 (Statement of Business or Professional Activities), which is included in your T1 General (personal income tax return). You report gross income, subtract eligible business expenses, and the net self-employment income is what gets taxed.
5
Pay CPP on your self-employment income. As a self-employed person, you pay both the employee (5.95%) and employer (5.95%) portions of CPP — roughly 11.9% on your net self-employment income. This is calculated automatically when you file using CRA-certified tax software.
6
File by June 15th — but pay by April 30th. Self-employed Canadians have until June 15 to file their return, but any balance owing is still due April 30. Late payment means interest charges from May 1 onward even if you file on time.
✅ Recommended Tools
Tax software: TurboTax Self-Employed, Wealthsimple Tax, or H&R Block — all handle T2125 and self-employment CPP automatically

Bookkeeping: A simple spreadsheet works. Track date, amount received, and what it was for. Export monthly.

Savings account: Open a separate savings account and auto-transfer 25–30% of every commission the day you receive it. Don't touch it until tax time.
⚠️ Important Disclaimer
This section is a general overview for Canadian independent contractors. Tax rules change and vary by province.

This is not professional tax advice. If you're unsure about your specific situation — especially around GST/HST registration, deductions, or installments — consult a CPA or tax professional. The cost of an accountant is far less than a CRA penalty.

The CRA's self-employment guide (RC4070) is also a free resource available at canada.ca.

About Northern Stay
What is Northern Stay?
▼
Northern Stay is a campground membership that gives you access to a growing network of campgrounds across Canada — without paying nightly camping fees. Instead of paying different prices every time you camp, you pay one clear membership price for the season. That means no fluctuating nightly rates, no peak-season sticker shock, and a much simpler way to plan your camping trips.

The fact that Lifetime Memberships can be transferred is sometimes what prompts the comparison — but the structure is completely different. There is no real estate involved, no property rights, and no ownership of land or campsites.

On the phone: "We're not a booking site — we're a membership. You pay once for the season and those nightly fees disappear. The sites you're booking aren't available to the general public either — they're reserved for members."

▼

🚨 The Urgency Language — Know It Cold

The invite page uses three distinct urgency/scarcity drivers. These are real and accurate — use them confidently.

Shown as a banner at the top. This exclusive invite is reserved just for them — once it expires or spots fill, this pricing may no longer be available.
On the call: "The page you'll get shows 'Limited Spots Available' — and that's not just marketing. We do personal invites for this reason. Once the window closes, your pricing goes up."

The page shows a countdown timer to a hard expiry date (e.g., "Offer expires Monday, March 23, 2026 — 13 days, 22 hours, 00 mins, 05 secs"). The clock is ticking in real time when they open the link.
On the call: "When you open your invite link, you'll actually see a countdown timer. That's the window for Founder pricing — after that date, the membership goes up significantly."

The page explicitly states: "Founder Membership spots are limited. If you don't book within your window, your spot will be offered to the next person on the waitlist.
